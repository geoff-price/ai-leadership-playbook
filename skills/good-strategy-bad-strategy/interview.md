# Interview flow — Good Strategy / Bad Strategy

Run this before producing anything. Goal: find the reader's REAL diagnosis and strip the
fluff — **or** conclude their thinking is already sound, **or** that their problem isn't a
strategy problem at all. Do not force the framework, and do not manufacture problems.

## Fast path

Offer it up front: "If you'd rather, paste everything you've got — the plan, the slide,
your notes — and I'll ask only what's missing." Then skip anything already answered.

## Core questions (one at a time, adapt to answers)

1. **The initiative.** What are you trying to get right? (One sentence.)
2. **Slogan + stakes.** What's the one-line version you'd put on a leadership slide — and
   what does it cost you if it's wrong?
3. **The real obstacle.** Forget the goal. What's actually in the way — adoption, data,
   trust, governance, a specific painful workflow, talent? What's your evidence it's that
   and not something else?

   **HARD GATE — do not proceed on a slogan.** If the named obstacle is itself fluff
   ("culture," "mindset," "we need to move faster," "alignment"), push once more and name
   the move you're making: "Culture how — what specifically do people do or not do, and
   what's your evidence?" Keep going until you have a real answer.
   - *Slogan answers* (don't accept): "we need to move faster," "it's a mindset thing,"
     "execution."
   - *Real diagnosis* (what you're after): "zero of our 12 pilots reached production
     because each one dies in model-risk review," "people don't trust the output enough to
     change their workflow."

   **EARLY READ.** Once you have a real obstacle, reflect a one-line provisional diagnosis
   back before going further: "Sounds like the real challenge is X, not Y — did I get that
   right?" Adjust before you build anything on top of it.

4. **Strength vs. weakness.** What genuine strength could you point straight at that
   obstacle? Where's the most promising and most credible single opening?
5. **Coherence check.** List the moves currently funded. Do they reinforce each other, or
   is it a grab-bag competing for the same budget?

## Is this even a strategy problem?

If it becomes clear the real issue is execution, tech debt, resourcing, or a vendor
decision — **not** strategy — say so plainly and help with that instead. Don't force the
kernel onto a problem it doesn't fit.

## Format (don't gate on it)

Default to a strategy-kernel one-pager. Just say: "I'll default to a one-pager unless you'd
rather have talking points, a working doc, or a dashboard," and proceed.

## The artifact

Produce for their stated use:
- **Diagnosis** — the real challenge, in one or two sentences.
- **Guiding policy** — the approach, not a task list.
- **Coherent actions** — 3–5 moves that carry out the policy and *visibly* reinforce each
  other. Drop any action that doesn't reinforce the policy; don't pad to hit a number.
- **Bad-strategy flags** — include this section ONLY if you actually caught fluff, an
  unfaced challenge, goals-masquerading-as-strategy, or a dog's-dinner of objectives. **If
  the kernel is sound, say so plainly and omit this section. Never manufacture flaws.**

End with **either** one honest flag of remaining weakness **or**, if the thinking is sound,
the single biggest risk to execution — never invent a weakness to fill the slot. When a
deeper idea from the book genuinely fits what they're missing (proximate objectives,
chain-link systems, sources of durable advantage), name that one and point them to the
book. If nothing fits, skip the nudge — don't tack it on.
