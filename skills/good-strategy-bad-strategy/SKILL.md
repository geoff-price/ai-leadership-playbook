# Good Strategy / Bad Strategy — for AI leaders

A decision tool built on one durable idea from *Good Strategy/Bad Strategy* by Richard
Rumelt. It interviews you about a real AI decision, then produces an artifact that turns
your slogans into an actual strategy — or shows you why you don't have one yet.

## The idea

Rumelt's core claim: most "strategies" are **bad strategy** — not the absence of strategy,
but a convincing imposter. It has four tells:

- **Fluff** — important-sounding language that says nothing ("leverage AI to drive
  transformational value").
- **Failure to face the challenge** — never naming the real obstacle, so nothing can be
  aimed at it.
- **Mistaking goals for strategy** — "grow adoption 30%" is a goal, not a method for
  getting there.
- **Bad strategic objectives** — goals that dodge the real problem: a scramble of
  disconnected priorities (Rumelt's "dog's dinner"), or "blue-sky" targets as hard to
  reach as the challenge itself.

Good strategy has a spine he calls the **kernel** — three parts:

1. **Diagnosis** — name the real challenge. Simplify the mess into a clear account of
   what's actually going on. ("Our problem isn't model access; it's that nobody trusts the
   output enough to change their workflow.")
2. **Guiding policy** — the overall approach you'll take to deal with that challenge. A
   direction, not a to-do list. ("Win trust first: deploy where we can show verifiable
   results and a human stays in control.")
3. **Coherent action** — concrete, coordinated moves that carry out the policy and
   reinforce one another (not a wishlist that pulls in five directions).

The translation for AI leadership: most AI roadmaps are bad strategy wearing a transformation
costume. They name outcomes ("AI-first," "20% productivity") without a diagnosis of the
real bottleneck or a guiding policy for overcoming it. Strategy, Rumelt says, is "the
application of strength against weakness" — your AI plan should point a real strength at a
real obstacle. This tool forces that.

## How to use this

1. Tell me the AI decision, roadmap, or initiative you're trying to get right.
2. I'll ask you a few sharp questions (see interview.md) to find your real diagnosis and
   strip the fluff.
3. I'll ask how you intend to use the result, then produce the right artifact — a strategy
   kernel one-pager, talking points, a working doc, or a dashboard — held to the standard
   in design-brief.md.

If your kernel turns out to be sound, I'll say so — I won't invent a problem to look
clever. And if the real issue isn't strategy at all (execution, tech debt, resourcing), I'll
tell you that instead of forcing the framework onto it.
